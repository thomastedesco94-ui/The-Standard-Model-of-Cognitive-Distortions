(For anyone who wants a clearer head and doesn't know where to start).

There isn’t a single, universally standardised list of cognitive distortions, but many are widely recognised in Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT). **Aaron T. Beck**, one of the founders of CBT, initially introduced the concept of cognitive distortions in the 1960s. His landmark work *Cognitive Therapy of Depression* (Beck et al., 1979) remains the foundational clinical text. His student **David D. Burns** later expanded on it in *Feeling Good: The New Mood Therapy* (1980). Most sources align on the main types of cognitive distortions but might phrase or categorise them slightly differently.

To avoid confusion, I’ve attempted to create a **unified, clear, and accessible reference** for cognitive distortions, essentially a “standard model” for the mind, inspired by the standard model of particle physics. It is also important to note that many thoughts contain **multiple cognitive distortions**, and some distortions overlap by design. The goal is **recognition, not perfect classification**; identifying even one distortion is sufficient for effective CBT work.

This model (The Standard Model of Cognitive Distortions) was designed to work with **Albert Ellis's** ABC framework, which grew out of his foundational work on Rational Emotive Behaviour Therapy (REBT) (Ellis, 1957), and which is now considered the foundational framework of all Cognitive Behavioural Therapies (CBT).

## A. Thinking in Extremes

### 1. All-or-Nothing Thinking (Binary Judgements)
- **Description:** When one sees situations, outcomes, or behaviours in black-and-white extremes, with no middle ground.
- **Example:** “If I don’t do this perfectly, I’ve failed.”

### 2. All-or-Nothing Thinking: Identity-Based Absolutes (Extreme Self-Ratings / Labelling)
- **Subtype of All-or-Nothing Thinking** 
- **Description:** When one turns specific mistakes or events into absolute judgments about themselves or others.
- **Example:** “I made a mistake, so I _am_ a failure.”
- **Other notes:** 
  - Overlaps with Overgeneralisation: Labelling and Mislabelling.
  - Overlaps with Always Being Right

### 3. Magnification
- **Description:** When one exaggerates the threat, significance, or impact of something. 
- **Example:** “This tiny mistake could ruin everything.”

### 4. Magnification: Catastrophising
- **Extreme subtype of Magnification**  
- **Description:** When one assumes the worst possible outcome or treats discomfort as catastrophic. 
- **Example:** “What if the absolute worst happens?”

### 5. Minimisation
- **Description:** When one downplays or dismisses a situation, achievement, or concern.  
- **Example:** “It’s not bullying, it’s just banter.”
- **Other notes:** 
  - Overlaps with Disqualifying the Positive

---

## B. Filtering and Biases / Assuming the Worst

### 1. Mental Filtering
- **Description:** When one dwells only on the negative details of a situation and ignores the positive aspects.
- **Example:** “Nothing good happened today.”  
- **Other notes:** 
  - Selective abstraction and confirmation bias can also fit here as subcategories.

### 2. Disqualifying the Positive
- **Description:** When one rejects positive experiences by insisting, they "don't count" for some reason or other.
- **Example:** “They didn’t really mean that, they were just being nice.”  
- **Other notes:**  
  - May be the most common distortion.    
  - Frequently associated with depressive thinking.
  - Overlaps with All-or-Nothing Thinking: Identity-Based Absolutes (Extreme Self-Ratings / Labelling)
  - Overlaps with Minimisation
  - Overlaps with Always Being Right
---

## C. Jumping to Conclusions

### 1. Mind Reading
- **Description:** When one assumes they know what others think.
- **Example:** “She won’t want to talk to me.”

### 2. Fortune Telling
- **Description:** When one predicts the future negatively. 
- **Example:** “I will fail.”

### 3. Overgeneralisation
- **Description:** When one sees one event as a permanent pattern.  
- **Example:** “Everyone dislikes me.”

### 4. Overgeneralisation: Labelling and Mislabelling
- **Extreme subtype of Overgeneralisation**  
- **Description:** When one assigns broad, usually negative, labels to oneself or others based on single instances or behaviours.  
- **Example:** “I’m a loser.”
- **Other notes:** 
  - Overlaps with All-or-Nothing Thinking: Identity-Based Absolutes (Extreme Self-Ratings / Labelling)

---

## D. Self and Other Misattributions

### 1. Personalisation
- **Description:** When one takes responsibility for events outside their control. 
- **Example:** “This happened because of me.”

### 2. Blaming Others
- **Description:** When one externalises responsibility.
- **Example:** “This is all their fault.”

### 3. Fallacy of Change
- **Description:** When one expects others to change to suit them.  
- **Example:** “Things will be better once he quits drinking.”  
- **Other notes:** Control fallacies (internal vs external control) can also fit here as subcategories.

### 4. Always Being Right
- **Description:** When one has rigid thinking about correctness.
- **Example:** “If I want it done properly, I’ll have to do it myself.”  
- **Other notes:** 
  - Overlaps with Rigid Rules
  - Overlaps with All-or-Nothing Thinking: Identity-Based Absolutes (Extreme Self-Ratings / Labelling)
  - Arguably behaviourally distinct, but Burns includes it (it's arguably more of a relational pattern than a thought distortion)

---

## E. Emotional Distortions

### 1. Emotional Reasoning
- **Description:** When one equates their feelings to facts.
- **Example:** “I feel useless, so I must be useless.”

### 2. Gratitude Traps
- **Description:** When one misunderstands the nature or practice of gratitude and uses it to justify expectations or entitlement. 
- **Example:** “I bought her a drink, so she should go on a date with me.”  
- **Other notes:**  
  - Modern concept, not part of classic CBT.  
  - Related to entitlement schemas, transactional thinking, or distortions in social cognition.

---

## F. Rigid Rules

### 1. Should / Shouldn’t / Must / Mustn’t Statements
- **Description:**  When one applies rigid, inflexible rules about how they, others, or the world should or must be. 
- **Examples:**  
  - “I shouldn’t have made so many mistakes.”  
  - “I must do better next time.”  
- **Other notes:** Clinically common distortion, deserves its own category.

# How to Use This Model in CBT (ABC Framework)

This model uses the **ABC framework**, a core structure in CBT:  
- **A - Activating Event:** What happened  
- **B - Belief (Automatic Thought):** What went through your mind  
- **C - Consequence:** How you felt and what you did  

The **Standard Model of Cognitive Distortions** is used at **B**.

### Step 1: Identify the Activating Event (A)
- Ask: “What happened just before I started feeling this way?”  
- Can be:  
  - Something external (a comment, mistake, rejection)  
  - Something internal (a memory, thought, or physical sensation)  
- **Example (A):** I made a mistake at work.

### Step 2: Identify the Belief / Automatic Thought (B)
- Ask: “What did I tell myself about this?”  
- Usually fast, emotional, and feels true.  
- **Example (B):** “I’ve failed again; I’ll never get my life together.”

### Step 3: Identify the Cognitive Distortion(s)
- Use the **Standard Model** to label distortions in the thought.  
- You only need to identify **one**.  
- **Example distortions:**  
  - All-or-Nothing Thinking  
  - Overgeneralisation  
  - Fortune Telling

Many thoughts contain multiple distortions. The goal is **recognition**, not perfect classification.

### Step 4: Challenge the Distorted Belief
- Ask evidence-based questions:  
  - “What is the evidence for and against this thought?”  
  - “Am I confusing feelings with facts?”  
  - “Is there a more balanced way to see this?”  
  - “What would I say to a friend in this situation?”

### Step 5: Reframe the Thought (Balanced Alternative)
- Replace the distorted belief with a more accurate, flexible thought (not forced positivity).  
- **Example reframe:** “I made a mistake, but that doesn’t define me. I’ve handled setbacks before and can do so again.”

### Step 6: Notice the Consequence (C)
- Observe any change in:  
  - Emotions  
  - Behaviours  
  - Physical sensations  

Even a small shift matters. CBT works through **practice and repetition**.
Regular journaling helps you spot patterns over time. Consider saving finished entries so you can look back and notice patterns and progress e.g. "It looks like I keep catastrophising about work specifically." Or "Oh nice, my mind reading has reduced over the past month."

---

## Key CBT Principle
- The goal of CBT is **not to eliminate negative thoughts**, but to **replace distorted beliefs with more accurate and flexible ones**.

# Worked Example - CBT Journal Entry
*2026-03-04*

## What happened
Something external (a comment, mistake, rejection)... I have been rejected for a job I really wanted. This isn't the first time this has happened.

## Automatic thought
I will never get the job I want. I am a failure.

## Thinking traps identified
- All-or-Nothing Thinking: Identity-Based Absolutes (Extreme Self-Ratings / Labelling)
- Magnification: Catastrophising
- Fortune Telling
- Overgeneralisation: Labelling and Mislabelling
- Personalisation
- Emotional Reasoning

## Challenge notes
What is the actual evidence for this thought?
Well, I have been rejected more than once, but I suppose it's more frustrating rather than proof I will never get the job I want...

## Balanced reframe
I do not know for certain if I will never get the job I want. Labelling myself as a failure might be a bit harsh. It's also possible they promoted someone internally or something, so the decision might not have been personal...

## FAQ
**Q: Is this a replacement for therapy?**

**A:** No. Please refer to the disclaimer as a reminder that “this is a psychoeducational framework, not a diagnostic or therapeutic tool. This framework does not replace working with a qualified therapist. If you are experiencing significant distress, please seek professional support.”

**Q: Is there evidence that self-guided CBT is effective?**

**A:** Yes! CBT has been shown to be clinically effective even when self-guided. For example, the meta analysis paper Computer-based psychological treatments for depression: A systematic review and meta-analysis (Richards & Richardson, 2012)

**Q: What are cognitive distortions?**

**A:** A cognitive distortion is a thought that causes a person to perceive reality inaccurately due to being exaggerated or irrational. Essentially, they refer to any “noise”* in our beliefs. Literally anything that may not be 100% accurate e.g. concluding uncertain things are certain rather than just likely or unlikely. 

For example, one time I got rejected for a job I had interviewed for and concluded, “I messed it up and will never get a job.” Later, the recruiter reached out to me, as they had requested additional feedback from the company and had finally heard back. Turns out they were actually convinced I could do the job, but due to budget issues, they had to put a pause on all hiring entirely. No one got the job. I ended up getting offered a very similar role shortly after this, as well as another invitation to interview somewhere else. 

*In experimental sciences, noise can refer to any random fluctuations of data that hinders perception of a signal. At the end of the day we’re all imperfect information processors. We pick up noise. CBT helps reduce it.

**Q: Is this just forced positivity or “toxic optimism”?**

**A:** No. CBT doesn’t change what happened or make bad things good. Think of it as “reducing noise”. For example, say the Activating Event (A) was that you did a bad thing.

The resulting Belief (B) (the Automatic Thought that went through your mind) might be something like “I’m awful and will never be forgiven!” This is loud, it drowns everything out and prevents any useful response. A less “noisy” more balanced thought would be something like “I did a bad thing but that doesn't define me, and it doesn't mean I can't stop doing bad things. I can try to apologise, and whilst I may not be forgiven by those I have harmed, I can accept accountability, avoid making things worse and try to be a better person going forward”. 

The Consequence (C) of this has not forced positivity and rejected accountability, it’s just “eliminated some noise” resulting in a reframed Belief (B) which is quieter and more accurate. Quieter thoughts leave room to actually deal with reality.

**A:** No. Select any that may apply, at least one, but you only need one to do good work! Many thoughts contain several. Remember the goal is recognition, not perfect classification.

**Q: Does the HTML version work on mobile?**

**A:** Yes. Please refer to the instructions on how to use the model for further details, including how to open the downloaded version on iPhone/iPad. 

**Q: At the end of the HTML exercise it asks us to score how we are feeling, wouldn't it make sense to also have this at the start so a comparison can be made?**

**A:** Starting an exercise like this by rating how bad you feel anchors you to your worst moment before you’ve had any chance to move. It also isn’t a great way to be greeted when you’re already struggling. The goal is to reduce noise first, then notice the shift. By requesting one to score themselves at this point is just a nudge to recognise the consequence. It is not necessary to measure the noise before and after like a clinical trial. Even a small change at step 6 is meaningful, and you’re more likely to notice it honestly if you haven’t already committed to a number that defines how bad things are.

**Q: Is any data I input into the HTML URL version collected?**

**A:** No. You have the option to save your entry locally at the end of the exercise but that's it.
