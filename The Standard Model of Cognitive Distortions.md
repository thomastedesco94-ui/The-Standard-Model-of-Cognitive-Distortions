(A working model attempt at a single, universally standardised list of cognitive distortions)

For anyone who wants a clearer head and doesn't know where to start.

**Disclaimer:** This is a psychoeducational framework, not a diagnostic or therapeutic tool. This framework does not replace working with a qualified therapist. If you are experiencing significant distress, please seek professional support. There isn’t a single, universally standardised list of cognitive distortions, but many are widely recognised in Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT). **Aaron T. Beck**, one of the founders of CBT, initially introduced the concept of cognitive distortions in the 1960s. His landmark work *Cognitive Therapy of Depression* (Beck et al., 1979) remains the foundational clinical text. His student **David D. Burns** later expanded on it in *Feeling Good: The New Mood Therapy* (1980). Most sources align on the main types of cognitive distortions but might phrase or categorise them slightly differently.

To avoid confusion, I’ve attempted to create a **unified, clear, and accessible reference** for cognitive distortions, essentially a “standard model” for the mind, inspired by the standard model of particle physics. It is also important to note that many thoughts contain **multiple cognitive distortions**, and some distortions overlap by design. The goal is **recognition, not perfect classification**; identifying even one distortion is sufficient for effective CBT work.

This model (The Standard Model of Cognitive Distortions) was designed to work with **Albert Ellis's** ABC framework, which grew out of his foundational work on Rational Emotive Behaviour Therapy (REBT) (Ellis, 1957), and which is now considered the foundational framework of all Cognitive Behavioural Therapies (CBT).

## A. Thinking in Extremes

### 1. All-or-Nothing Thinking (Binary Judgements)
- **Description:** When one sees situations, outcomes, or behaviours in black-and-white extremes, with no middle ground.
- **Example:** “If I don’t do this perfectly, I’ve failed.”

### 2. All-or-Nothing Thinking: Identity-Based Absolutes (Extreme Self-Ratings / Labelling)
- **Subtype of All-or-Nothing Thinking** 
- **Description:** When one turns specific mistakes or events into absolute judgments about themselves or others.
- **Example:** “I made a mistake, so I _am_ a failure.”
- **Other notes:** 
  - Overlaps with Overgeneralisation: Labelling and Mislabelling.
  - Overlaps with Always Being Right

### 3. Magnification
- **Description:** When one exaggerates the threat, significance, or impact of something. 
- **Example:** “This tiny mistake could ruin everything.”

### 4. Magnification: Catastrophising
- **Extreme subtype of Magnification**  
- **Description:** When one assumes the worst possible outcome or treats discomfort as catastrophic. 
- **Example:** “What if the absolute worst happens?”

### 5. Minimisation
- **Description:** When one downplays or dismisses a situation, achievement, or concern.  
- **Example:** “It’s not bullying, it’s just banter.”
- **Other notes:** 
  - Overlaps with Disqualifying the Positive

## B. Filtering and Biases / Assuming the Worst

### 1. Mental Filtering
- **Description:** When one dwells only on the negative details of a situation and ignores the positive aspects.
- **Example:** “Nothing good happened today.”  
- **Other notes:** 
  - Selective abstraction and confirmation bias can also fit here as subcategories.

### 2. Disqualifying the Positive
- **Description:** When one rejects positive experiences by insisting, they "don't count" for some reason or other.
- **Example:** “They didn’t really mean that, they were just being nice.”  
- **Other notes:**  
  - May be the most common distortion.    
  - Frequently associated with depressive thinking.
  - Overlaps with All-or-Nothing Thinking: Identity-Based Absolutes (Extreme Self-Ratings / Labelling)
  - Overlaps with Minimisation
  - Overlaps with Always Being Right

## C. Jumping to Conclusions

### 1. Mind Reading
- **Description:** When one assumes they know what others think.
- **Example:** “She won’t want to talk to me.”

### 2. Fortune Telling
- **Description:** When one predicts the future negatively. 
- **Example:** “I will fail.”

### 3. Overgeneralisation
- **Description:** When one sees one event as a permanent pattern.  
- **Example:** “Everyone dislikes me.”

### 4. Overgeneralisation: Labelling and Mislabelling
- **Extreme subtype of Overgeneralisation**  
- **Description:** When one assigns broad, usually negative, labels to oneself or others based on single instances or behaviours.  
- **Example:** “I’m a loser.”
- **Other notes:** 
  - Overlaps with All-or-Nothing Thinking: Identity-Based Absolutes (Extreme Self-Ratings / Labelling)

---

## D. Self and Other Misattributions

### 1. Personalisation
- **Description:** When one takes responsibility for events outside their control. 
- **Example:** “This happened because of me.”

### 2. Blaming Others
- **Description:** When one externalises responsibility.
- **Example:** “This is all their fault.”

### 3. Fallacy of Change
- **Description:** When one expects others to change to suit them.  
- **Example:** “Things will be better once he quits drinking.”  
- **Other notes:** Control fallacies (internal vs external control) can also fit here as subcategories.

### 4. Always Being Right
- **Description:** When one has rigid thinking about correctness.
- **Example:** “If I want it done properly, I’ll have to do it myself.”  
- **Other notes:** 
  - Overlaps with Rigid Rules
  - Overlaps with All-or-Nothing Thinking: Identity-Based Absolutes (Extreme Self-Ratings / Labelling)
  - Arguably behaviourally distinct, but Burns includes it (it's arguably more of a relational pattern than a thought distortion)

---

## E. Emotional Distortions

### 1. Emotional Reasoning
- **Description:** When one equates their feelings to facts.
- **Example:** “I feel useless, so I must be useless.”

### 2. Gratitude Traps
- **Description:** When one misunderstands the nature or practice of gratitude and uses it to justify expectations or entitlement. 
- **Example:** “I bought her a drink, so she should go on a date with me.”  
- **Other notes:**  
  - Modern concept, not part of classic CBT.  
  - Related to entitlement schemas, transactional thinking, or distortions in social cognition.

---

## F. Rigid Rules

### 1. Should / Shouldn’t / Must / Mustn’t Statements
- **Description:**  When one applies rigid, inflexible rules about how they, others, or the world should or must be. 
- **Examples:**  
  - “I shouldn’t have made so many mistakes.”  
  - “I must do better next time.”  
- **Other notes:** Clinically common distortion, deserves its own category.
